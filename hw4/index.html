Overview

This homework, although being the last of assignment of the Computer Graphic class, is my build of a real time cloth simulation. The simulation is based on the foundation of the mass spring system. In short, point masses make up the mass spring system and are connected by three types, structural, shearing, and bending springs. These springs fall and fold under gravity like real springs, collide with primitives, and avoid clipping through themeselves by spatial hashing and thanks to math. Finally, to give the cloth a realistic look GPU shaders handle lighting from diffuse to environment mapped reflections. Plus, I created a custom holographic shader!

Masses & Springs

Part 1 · Building the Grid

The cloth is represented into the grid system via num_width_points × num_height_points grid of point masses stored in row-major order. For the vertical orientation, point masses are placed over the XY plane with a small random Z offset between ±1/1000. The z offset is small random value that won't affect vertical placement. For horizontal orientation, Y is fixed at 1 and the grid spans the XZ plane. Y does not have an offset here because it will always stay at a constant 1 for horizontal placement.

Mentioned in the overview, springs connect neighboring masses in three types.

  • Structural springs connect each mass to the one directly to its left and directly above it.
  • Shearing springs connect diagonally to the upper-left and upper-right neighbors, resisting skewing forces.
  • Bending springs skip one mass, connecting two to the left and two above, resisting folding.
Notice! For my implementation, all point masses are added before any springs, since springs store pointers into the point_masses vector. Adding springs during mass insertion risks pointer invalidation if the vector reallocates.

Numerical Integration

Part 2 · Simulating Physics

For this step, it's important to understand how each timestep affects the positions. Each timestep applies forces then intergrates each positions to apply the force in the simulation. For example a type of forces:

  • External accelerations (gravity) are summed and multiplied by mass.

Spring correction forces follow Hooke's law! Hooke's law is applied by bending springs use a 20% reduced spring constant to model weaker fold resistance.

\[ F_s = k_s \cdot (|\mathbf{p}_a - \mathbf{p}_b| - l) \]

Another necessary formula to get velocity to accurately adjust density of a cloth is Verlet integration. It derives velocity from position history with a damping term. A deformation constraint ensures no spring stretches more than 10% beyond rest length per timestep. A way that helped me understand Verlet integration is to see it as a GPS. Verlet is like a GPS that calculates your speed from two position readings. By doing so, it derives velocity from where you were vs where you are now. Also, the deformation constraint is like two people holding a rubber band that's stretched too far. If you push them toward each other along the same line without changing direction.

\[ \mathbf{x}_{t+dt} = \mathbf{x}_t + (1-d)(\mathbf{x}_t - \mathbf{x}_{t-dt}) + \frac{\mathbf{F}}{m} \cdot dt^2 \]

Parameter observations

  • Low ks makes the cloth elastic and droopy, taking longer to swing back and forth like a pendulum, before settling.
  • High ks is stiff and barely deforms. Higher density increases gravitational effect.
  • Low damping causes longer oscillation, swinging back and more continously without stopping.
  • High damping settles immediately without bounce.

Collisions

Part 3 · Spheres & Planes

For my implementation to hande sphere collisions, I check if a point mass falls inside the sphere by comparing distance to the radius. First, I had to understand what I am checking for. The visual example of a flashlight beam exiting a glass ball, insteead of a prism, helped. If you extend the ray from the center through the point to the surface is the tangent point on the sphere my implementation finds. If inside, I find the tangent point by extending the direction from origin through the mass to the sphere surface, compute a correction from last position to that tangent, and lastly apply it scaled by friction.

For plane collisions, in my implementation I detect collisions by crossing. Specifically checking if the dot product of (position − plane point) with the normal changes sign between timesteps. An example I used to visualize this process is "quantum tunneling". The chance a particle phases through solid walls, the particle checked if its on the other side of the wall. In this case the plane is the wall and the cloth is the particle. I find the crossing using linear interpolation \( t = d_{last} / (d_{last} - d_{current}) \), place the tangent slightly above the surface using SURFACE_OFFSET, and apply the correction scaled by friction.

Self-Collision

Part 4 · Spatial Hashing

Unless you have a super computer handy on you, naive O(n²) pair-checking is too slow for real-time simulation! In my implementation I use spatial hashing! At each timestep I partition 3D space into box volumes and hash each point mass into its box. Only bucket-mates point masses are checked for collision. Below is the formula for the hashing.

\[ \text{hash} = x_{box} \cdot p_1 + y_{box} \cdot p_2 + z_{box} \cdot p_3 \]

Box dimensions use \(w = 3 \cdot \text{width} / \text{num\_width\_points}\), \(h = 3 \cdot \text{height} / \text{num\_height\_points}\), \(t = \max(w, h)\). To ensure unqiue keys I decided to use large 7 digit prime multipliers. These prime numbers are stored as floats and make the resulting float per box in the 3D grid. An edge case to keep in mid is if a pair of bucket-mates is within \(2 \times \text{thickness}\), a repulsion correction is applied, averaged across all pairs, and scaled by simulation_steps.

Shaders

Part 5 · GLSL on the GPU

Before this class I only understood shaders made Minecraft beautiful. From adding sunlight rays and beautiful water physics. Now I understand how shaders create such graphics and where they run. Shaders are small isolated programs running in parallel on the GPU! While running in parallel there is a vertex to fragment pipeline, math goes in (vertex) and pixel color comes out. Vertex shaders execute once per vertex, transforming geometry and passing per-vertex data to the fragment shader via varyings. Fragment shaders execute once per pixel, computing a final color using interpolated data and uniforms. This separation lets the GPU process geometry and color simultaneously at massive scale.

Task 2 — Blinn-Phong

There are three components that are crucial for Blinn-Phong shading

  • Ambient lighting prevents pure black on the object.
  • Diffuse models matte scattering for texture.
  • Specular adds a shiny highlight using halfway vector \(\mathbf{h}\) between light and view directions, raised to shininess exponent \(p\)
\[ L = k_a I_a + k_d \frac{I}{r^2} \max(0, \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{l}) + k_s \frac{I}{r^2} \max(0, \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{h})^p \]

Task 4 — Bump & Displacement Mapping

Bump mapping affects surface normals using height map finite differences. By creating a difference in the heigh map, bump mapping still maintains a local normal \(\mathbf{n}_o = (-dU, -dV, 1)\) transformed to world space via the TBN matrix. The sphere's and cloth's silhouette stays smooth. It gives the illusion of texturem, displacement does not.

Displacement mapping physically moves vertices along the normal scaled by height. Changing the vertices affects real geometry on the sphere and cloth. The changes are visible in the silhouette and at low mesh resolution. There is no illusion on the sphere nor the cloth. If you could touch the simulation, you would feel the ridges.

At -o 16 -a 16, bump mapping keeps a smooth silhouette while displacement shows visible polygon faceting. The fundamental difference between faking normals and moving actual geometry.

Task 5 — Mirror / Environment Map

Mirrors reflect what we see in our world, so that is exactly what I needed to calculate. My implementation computes the outgoing eye ray, reflects it across the surface normal using GLSL's built-in reflect(). Then samples the cubemap in that reflected direction, approximating mirror reflections without ray tracing! Saving our computers a lot of render time, and still getting a high quality mirror finish via approximation.

Holographic Bubble Shader Extra Credit

Custom Shader · Iridescent Fresnel + Dual Specular

My goal was to simulate the visual behavior of a soap bubble and a black pearl! Something with a dark glassy interior, a shifting rainbow rim, and two sharp specular glints like light catching a curved surface.

The process

Starting from a blank Custom.frag, I built the shader in three layers. The foundation is a Fresnel term based on Schlick's 1994 approximation: the angle between the surface normal and view direction determines how "edge-on" each pixel is. Pixels at the grazing edge get a high fresnel value of 1, pixels facing the camera directly get 0.

\[ f = 1 - \max(0, \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{v}) \]

That fresnel value feeds into Inigo Quilez's cosine palette formula to generate the rainbow rim color. The formula cycles through hue space as fresnel increases from 0 to 1, creating a smooth spectrum at the edges:

\[ \text{rainbow}(t) = 0.5 + 0.5 \cdot \cos(6.28 \cdot (t + \langle 0.0, 0.33, 0.67 \rangle)) \]

The diffuse color is then rainbow_color * fresnel². I need to square fresnel pushes color strongly to the edges and keeps the center dark like a real bubble. On top of this I layered the cubemap environment reflection blended by fresnel, a dark blue base to prevent pure black, and two specular highlights: the primary from the scene light and a secondary from a hardcoded fake light position to create the double-glint effect characteristic of glass spheres.

Citations

Schlick, C. (1994). An Inexpensive BRDF Model for Physically-based Rendering. Computer Graphics Forum, 13(3), 233–246. — Fresnel approximation concept.

Quilez, I. (2017). Palettes. iquilezles.org. — Cosine palette color cycling formula.

The rainbow rim emerges purely from math!!! :) No texture lookup needed. The Fresnel term encodes viewing angle, the cosine palette maps that to hue, and squaring the Fresnel term confines color to the grazing edges exactly like a real soap bubble!
